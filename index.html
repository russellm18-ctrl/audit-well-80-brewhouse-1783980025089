## Atmospheric Reality Gap

Your headline promise is "WELL MADE BEER AND GREAT FOOD." What your hero images actually deliver is a mixed signal that undercuts that claim at the moment it matters most.

The food shot — a cheeseburger with fries — has competent close-up focus and warm amber tones that make the cheese pull read clearly. That frame is doing work. But the black-and-white chef portrait overlaid on top of it kills the appetite signal entirely. Desaturating your food environment tells a buyer's nervous system that nothing here is warm, fresh, or ready to eat. The monochrome treatment may be intended to communicate craft and character; what it actually communicates is that you are uncertain which story you are telling.

The brewery imagery compounds the problem. The fermentation tank shot is technically sharp but emotionally cold — industrial steel with no human warmth and no sensory pull. The pint glass inset is the weakest frame in the set: flat ambient light, no condensation, no pour motion, no table context. It reads like a stock photo used to fill space rather than an image built to sell a specific beer brewed from Olympia's artesian water. Your copy names a legendary brewing lineage going back to 1895. Your beer photography doesn't know that story exists.

The third image — vintage Olympia postcards — introduces a completely different visual register: illustrative, retro, low-resolution archival art. Placed alongside food and beer photography, it signals "we grabbed what we had." The trust cost: a group deciding between two brewpubs for a Saturday night looks at your homepage and sees a brand that hasn't committed to what it is.

## Visual Narrative Coherence

Your single most expensive fracture is `https://well80.com/beer-menu/` returning a 404. Your homepage copy links to that URL twice — once in the artesian water origin story, once in the "Enjoy a Drink" section. Every buyer who reads about your "award-winning tap list" and clicks to see it hits a dead page. That is not a minor technical issue. That is your highest-intent buyer, mid-decision, bouncing to a competitor whose beer menu loads.

The gift card pathway is broken in two separate places: `https://well80.cardfoundry.com/giftcards.php` returns 404 and `https://www.toasttab.com/well80/giftcards` returns 403. Gift card buyers are high-value, low-friction conversions — they have already decided to spend money with you. Both routes to that transaction are closed.

Your online ordering link at `https://www.toasttab.com/well80/v3` also returns 403. A buyer who finishes reading your food copy and wants to order is hitting a wall. Four broken links out of 25 checked is a 16% failure rate on homepage navigation. High-value buyers comparing vendors read that as operational neglect.

## Metadata and Conversion Gaps

You rank #1 organically for "Well 80 Brewhouse" — that ranking is not your problem. Your problem is what happens after the click. Your meta description is 105 characters. That is functional but leaves room on the search result card that competitors in the "Olympia brewery" category can use to out-narrate you before anyone arrives at your site.

One of your nine homepage images — 11% — is missing alt text. In a food and beverage context, image alt text is not an accessibility checkbox; it is how Google reads your menu items, your beer names, and your food descriptions as indexable content. Every unnamed image is a keyword you are not ranking for.

Your og:image is present, which means social shares pull a preview image — that is correct. But the image being pulled is the same visually inconsistent set audited above. A share of your homepage on Facebook or Instagram is the first impression for a referred buyer, and it is delivering the same fractured signal.

Your fourth hero image rendered as a blank white frame — no image loaded, no alt text, no content. That is a failed asset serving as a homepage element. Buyers don't know why it's blank. They just see a site that doesn't finish loading.
